# Methodology selection

Choose the smallest valid set of methods. Use triangulation in every project.

| Research question | Primary methods | Use when | Avoid when |
|---|---|---|---|
| Macro/category change | PESTEL, STEEP, lifecycle, scenarios | Regulation, technology, culture, or category structure matters | The question is a narrow campaign audit |
| Competitive pressure | Five Forces, strategic groups, value chain | Industry economics and bargaining power matter | Only consumer perception is needed |
| Business model | Business Model Canvas, value proposition, VRIO, unit economics | Public operating and financial evidence exists | Internal economics would be invented |
| Growth choices | Ansoff, BCG/GE, white-space analysis | Products, markets, and portfolio evidence are comparable | Portfolio data is incomplete |
| Audience definition | STP, U&A, occasion segmentation, RFM/cohorts | Survey, CRM, commerce, or observable behavior exists | Only demographic stereotypes exist |
| Consumer motivation | Jobs to Be Done, laddering, means?end chain, interviews | Explain why people choose, reject, or switch | Only aggregate counts exist |
| Experience | Customer Journey, Moments of Truth, service blueprint, mystery shopping | Touchpoints and service drive choice | There is no meaningful service journey |
| Brand positioning | 3C, POP/POD, perceptual mapping, positioning statement | Compare brand, needs, and competitors | Dimensions are selected without evidence |
| Brand equity | Aaker, Keller CBBE, brand funnel, association network | Diagnose awareness, meaning, response, loyalty | No audience or tracking evidence exists |
| Mental availability | Category Entry Points, distinctive assets, share of search | Memory cues and buying situations matter | Search volume is used as market share |
| Brand identity | Kapferer prism, brand onion/key, personality, archetypes | Decode coherent identity and expression | Archetypes replace evidence-based positioning |
| Social conversation | Social listening, netnography, coding, sentiment/topics | Natural consumer language is required | Samples are presented as population estimates |
| Culture/creative codes | Semiotics, discourse, narrative, cultural tension, weak signals | Symbols, aesthetics, subculture, or trends matter | Only isolated examples exist |
| Communications | PESO, IMC, message house, content pillars, touchpoints | Audit messages, channels, and consistency | Outcomes are attributed without causal evidence |
| Effectiveness | Brand lift, pre/post, incrementality, MMM, MTA, funnel | Suitable experiment, tracking, or spend data exists | Public correlations are treated as causal |
| Product/creative validation | Concept tests, MaxDiff, conjoint, A/B, monadic tests | Alternatives can be tested with a defined sample | No respondents or exposure data exists |
| Pricing | Van Westendorp, Gabor-Granger, conjoint | Valid respondent-level price research exists | Social comments alone set price |
| Reputation/crisis | Stakeholders, issue lifecycle, risk matrix, SCCT, red-team | Risk, controversy, or response readiness matters | Facts and authority are absent |

## Standard mixed-method sequence

1. Desk research establishes facts and hypotheses.
2. Social listening and netnography reveal natural language and emerging themes.
3. Qualitative research explains motivations when access exists.
4. Quantitative research estimates prevalence when a valid sample exists.
5. Commercial data tests whether perceptions correspond to behavior.
6. Triangulation determines confidence and contradictions.

Qualitative explains why; quantitative estimates how many; social listening observes what is expressed; commercial data measures realized behavior.
